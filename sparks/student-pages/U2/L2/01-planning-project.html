Planning Your Interactive Project

In this activity, you will plan an interactive Snap! project and seek input from other people to improve your plan.
You first learned about sprites, costumes, and the Snap! stage in Unit 1 Lab 4 Activity 3: Investigating and Storing Images.

Your project should have characters or objects that move around (sprites) on top of a background image (the Snap! stage). You'll control how the sprites look by changing their costumes, and you'll control how the stage looks by changing its background.

Here are a few of the costumes included in Snap!. You can also import your own.

  • A sprite is like an actor who can do many different things, such as move, talk, follow other sprites, and whatever else you program it to do!
  • A costume is a picture that can be "worn" by a sprite.

Here are a few of the backgrounds included in Snap!. You import your own backgrounds too.

  • The stage is the big rectangle in the upper right of the Snap! window. (It's white when you first open Snap!.) The stage is what users of your project see, and it's where your sprites live.
  • A background is a picture that can be "worn" by the stage.

New Snap! Skills

You're going to create an interactive project (such as a poster, a card, or a cartoon) using each of the following new skills you will build in this lab:

  1. Costumes and Backgrounds: You can use the ones built-in to Snap!, import pictures from the Internet, or draw your own.
  2. Animation: You'll make your sprites move around the stage, say things, and change costumes.
  3. Importing Code: You'll incorporate blocks from prior projects.
  4. Sounds: You'll add or create audio recordings.
  5. Events: You'll make your code perform certain actions (such as playing a sound or moving) when certain things happen (such as when the user clicks a sprite or when something else has finished happening).
  6. Hot Spots: You'll create special areas on the stage where the user can click to produce an specific action.

Making Your Plan

  1. Decide on the purpose and design of your interactive project. Will it serve as a poster, a greeting card, a cartoon, or something else? Who is it for? Is it for fun or for learning? Is there a theme (holiday, sports, colors, etc.)?

    6. Why gather feedback?

    Have you ever used an app or a website and thought that it was annoying or hard to use? The developers may not have had those problems when used it, but you are a different person! Designing good products requires getting input from different kinds of people to make sure that users have a good experience.
